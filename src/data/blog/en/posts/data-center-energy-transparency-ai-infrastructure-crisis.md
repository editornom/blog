## From the Model Parameter Race to the War for Megawatts (MW)

**![An editorial illustration depicting a massive, futuristic data center integrated into a power grid through transparent glass layers glowing with blue light, showing current and flow meters to symbolize transparency and energy monitoring.](../../../../../source/posts/데이터센터_에너지_투명성(Energy_Transparency)_공시_의무화와_AI_인프라_확장성_위기/aee0ead5-0.png)**

Until just a year or two ago, the primary mission of the AI industry was to develop models with larger parameters or to secure NVIDIA GPUs ahead of the competition. However, as of 2026, the market's center of gravity has completely shifted. The essence of AI competition is no longer just model design capability; it now depends on how reliably a company can secure "megawatt-scale" power to support it. This is why analysts suggest that the success of next-generation AI infrastructure depends on energy supply chain management rather than computational sophistication.

The recent case of xAI's "Colossus" data center in Memphis, Tennessee, symbolically illustrates this shift. When the speed of power grid expansion couldn't keep up with the data center construction, xAI took the drastic measure of "temporary self-generation" using gas turbines. However, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) intervened by reclassifying these as power generation facilities subject to strict regulation. This signifies more than just tightened environmental regulations; it marks the beginning of the "Energy Transparency" era, where data centers must transparently prove the entire process of how they procure and consume energy.

## Energy Transparency Disclosure: A New Prerequisite for Data Center Operations

**![A sleek digital dashboard display on a large screen inside a corporate office, including real-time graphs showing PUE and WUE metrics, carbon emissions, and energy procurement status from renewable sources, captured in a clean architectural photography style.](../../../../../source/posts/데이터센터_에너지_투명성(Energy_Transparency)_공시_의무화와_AI_인프라_확장성_위기/87b5921f-1.png)**

Moving forward, operating a data center in the global market will require more than just paying the bills; operators must disclose the origin and efficiency of their power in detail. This is the core of "mandatory energy transparency disclosure." While PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) was once merely a marketing tool, companies are now required to report their fuel mix, carbon emissions, and Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) for cooling.

Meeting these requirements technically demands a highly sophisticated Data Center Infrastructure Management (DCIM) system. To track power and environmental data in real-time, tens of thousands of sensor data points must be integrated and managed using communication protocols like **SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol)** or **BACnet**. Specifically, as seen in the xAI case, the ability to prove through data that pollution mitigation devices—such as **SCR (Selective Catalytic Reduction)**—are actually operational will determine a company's credibility.

This mandatory disclosure directly impacts corporate management.
> "Energy transparency is now a core element of Governance (G), moving beyond mere figures in a report. It has become a Social (S) prerequisite for reaching consensus with local communities and securing business permits."

Companies that fail to prove transparency may find it difficult to secure new sites or even expand existing facilities, posing a significant risk of stagnating the growth of their entire AI service portfolio.

## Technical Responses to Surging Power Density in Infrastructure

**![An editorial style combining 3D rendering and realistic industrial photography, overlaying a technical diagram highlighting liquid cooling pipes and 800V DC power distribution units over actual high-density server racks.](../../../../../source/posts/데이터센터_에너지_투명성(Energy_Transparency)_공시_의무화와_AI_인프라_확장성_위기/38ecfbb1-2.png)**

While energy regulations are tightening, the power density of AI servers is rising sharply. While traditional data center racks had a power density of 10–15 kW, AI-specific racks housing the latest GPU clusters are reaching 80–120 kW. Traditional air cooling is insufficient to handle the massive heat generated in such confined spaces.

To solve this scalability crisis, the role of professional infrastructure solutions like HaiOnNet is becoming increasingly emphasized. HaiOnNet provides a high-availability environment that efficiently manages complex data center networks and power monitoring systems. Specifically, optimized circuit configurations—which intelligently distribute network loads to increase power efficiency or minimize energy consumption during data transmission—can be a practical countermeasure in the era of transparency disclosure.

Technically, Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) for direct procurement of renewable energy are spreading to lower the **LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy)**. However, to compensate for the intermittency of renewables, efforts are being made to link large-scale Energy Storage Systems (ESS) or convert internal data center grids to 800V DC (direct current) distribution to reduce transformation losses. Such detailed technical responses are necessary to clear the regulatory hurdles.

## Geographical Constraints and the Shift Toward Distributed AI Infrastructure

**![A map of a geographical area with glowing nodes representing distributed edge data centers connected by high-speed fiber optic lines, implemented in a minimalist data visualization style for a business magazine.](../../../../../source/posts/데이터센터_에너지_투명성(Energy_Transparency)_공시_의무화와_AI_인프라_확장성_위기/5bcb9342-3.png)**

Traditional data center hubs like Northern Virginia, Dublin, and Amsterdam have already reached the limits of their power grid capacity. In many cases, it takes more than five years just to connect to a new transmission grid. The infrastructure scalability problem has expanded beyond technology into geographical and political territory.

Future AI infrastructure is likely to move away from massive campus formats and toward distributed models in regions with surplus power and easy access to renewable energy. This is the "Power-first Site Selection" strategy. The emergence of Johor, Malaysia, or Batam, Indonesia, as new AI hubs—circumventing Singapore’s regulations—can be understood in this context.

Companies need to prepare for these structural changes by:
*   **Reviewing Self-Generation Capabilities:** To reduce reliance on the central grid, companies should consider securing their own power sources using Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) or fuel cells.
*   **Energy-Efficiency Centric Architecture:** Adopting "Green AI" algorithm optimization to reduce power consumption per computation should be a design standard.
*   **Network Optimization:** For organic data transmission between distributed data centers, companies should resolve operational complexity caused by geographical dispersion by utilizing high-quality dedicated lines and security solutions provided by **HaiOnNet**.

## Practical Proposals for Sustainable AI Growth

**![A high-tech command center where engineers monitor wall screens showing real-time global energy and network status, cinematic editorial photography style with soft blue and amber lighting.](../../../../../source/posts/데이터센터_에너지_투명성(Energy_Transparency)_공시_의무화와_AI_인프라_확장성_위기/a9dff31b-4.png)**

It is rare to see a period where physical constraints so directly regulate the speed of technological advancement. Energy transparency disclosure for data centers is not just a regulation; it is a demand for the AI industry to adopt a sustainable structure.

From a practical standpoint, the first thing to check is the system for securing real-time energy data. Beyond simply aggregating total power usage, infrastructure must be equipped to monitor and digitize detailed metrics, including rack-level efficiency and cooling water recycling rates.

Furthermore, network and security partnerships must be redefined to solve infrastructure scalability issues. As infrastructure grows, network quality for stable operation becomes as vital as power. Collaborating with partners like HaiOnNet to reduce operational complexity and increase system availability will be a realistic alternative.

Finally, companies must consider coexistence with local communities. If data centers are perceived as monopolizing resources, business continuity cannot be guaranteed. Transparent information disclosure is the most reliable way to dispel community concerns and grow together. Ultimately, the true winners of the AI era will not be those with the largest models, but those with the most efficient and transparent infrastructure management capabilities.
