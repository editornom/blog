The global tech industry's gaze is shifting rapidly from hardware expansion to the stage of practical application. The era when NVIDIA's GPU sales or the scale of data center investments were the sole measures of innovation has passed. As of 2025, the market is no longer just looking at who owns the best models, but how deeply those models have penetrated industrial sites and daily life. Moving beyond 'Phase 1'—the construction of technology—the "Usage-driven AI Economy," where actual utilization determines economic value, has moved into full swing.

This paradigm means that real-world utilization per capita and the penetration rate within business processes now dictate the substantial competitiveness of nations and corporations. Recent data released by the likes of Microsoft and Anthropic show that this shift in economic structure is unfolding in directions we might not have expected.

### The Digital Divide Behind the 16.3% Penetration Rate

According to Microsoft's latest report, the global adoption rate of generative AI tools reached 16.3% as of the second half of 2025. This means one in six people worldwide is utilizing AI in their daily lives, a figure that shows a moderate upward trend compared to the first half of the year. However, behind this popularization lies a serious imbalance.

- The adoption rate in the Global North reached 24.7%, while the Global South remains at 14.1%.
- More concerning is that the gap between nations is widening, increasing from 9.8 percentage points to 10.6 percentage points.

As high-income countries with the capacity for infrastructure investment begin to monopolize the virtuous cycle of productivity through AI, technology is acting as a tool that deepens the divide rather than bridging it.

### The Reversal: Model Developers vs. Utilization Leaders

An interesting point in these statistics is that the United States, which leads in core technology and infrastructure, is falling behind in actual usage metrics. The U.S. ranked 24th in the world with an AI usage rate of 28.3% among the working-age population. In contrast, the United Arab Emirates (UAE) and Singapore took 1st and 2nd place, recording 64.0% and 60.9%, respectively.

South Korea’s progress is particularly noteworthy. Korea’s ranking surged from 25th in the first half to 18th in the second half. This suggests that AI has moved beyond a mere trend and has quickly settled into public services, education, and corporate environments. This is the combined result of massive government investment in education, the advancement of Korean-specific models, and major global companies securing hubs within the country. It proves that the capability to develop technology and the capability to establish it as a tool for economic agents are entirely separate domains.

### Open Source Breaking the Barriers of Cost

The biggest barrier to widespread usage has been cost. However, the growth of open-source platforms is drastically lowering this threshold. In particular, China's DeepSeek is receiving an explosive response in African and Middle Eastern markets, where financial and technical constraints were previously high.

- China is increasing its market share by forming strategic partnerships with local infrastructure companies, spearheaded by DeepSeek.
- This signifies that the competition for technological hegemony has transformed from a battle of pure performance to a battle of who can secure more users at a lower cost.

Now, securing the authority to preempt user habits has become as important as data sovereignty.

### The Dilemma: Productivity Bonus vs. Replaceability

A survey conducted by Anthropic of over 80,000 users clearly shows the impact of AI utilization on the workplace. The higher the AI exposure of a job, the more substantial the productivity gains experienced; however, job insecurity rose proportionately. Early-career professionals, in particular, felt a higher sense of crisis than senior-level employees.

The paradox is that respondents whose work speed increased rapidly due to AI recognized their own replaceability more strongly. As efficiency is maximized, concerns are surfacing that the scarcity of individual expertise may decrease. This poses a new challenge for the labor market: productivity gains do not necessarily lead to job security.

### A Reality Check for Creating Real Value

We are now at a testing ground where infrastructure fueled by massive capital must prove its practical economic value. A true usage-driven economy does not mean simply trying out generative tools a few times. It refers to a state where AI operates as a substantial engine that contributes to a nation's GDP, improves corporate operating profit structures, and redefines the value of individual labor.

![Usage-based AI Economics - A graph showing the transition to an application-centric era as infrastructure investment stabilizes and usage-based economic value surges.](../../../../../source/posts/사용량_기반_AI_경제_(Usage-based_AI_Economics)/21add2b1-2.webp)

The winners of the future will not be the countries that built the largest models, but the countries and companies that most cleverly put tools into people's hands and enabled them to use them. The localized services and active public intervention shown by Korea, along with the cost-efficiency demonstrated by open source, will be the key variables for success. It is time to peel back the technical illusions and soberly assess how precisely our society's workflows are synchronized with this massive economic engine.
